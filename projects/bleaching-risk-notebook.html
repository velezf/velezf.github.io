Overview

When I started looking for a dataset to anchor this first marine science project, I had a simple question: what data actually gets used by the people trying to protect coral reefs in the real world? That question led me directly to NOAA’s Coral Reef Watch (CRW) program and their Degree Heating Weeks (DHW) product — and then to the paper that describes exactly how it’s built: Skirving et al. (2020), CoralTemp and the Coral Reef Watch Coral Bleaching Heat Stress Product Suite Version 3.1 (Skirving et al. 2020).

Reading that paper changed how I thought about this project.

Data source: NOAA Coral Reef Watch Version 3.1 Daily 5km Satellite Coral Bleaching Heat Stress Product Suite. Updated daily. coralreefwatch.noaa.gov

Why not just use raw sea surface temperature?

My first instinct was to pull raw SST data — it’s widely available from multiple sources including NOAA OISST, NASA MODIS, and the Copernicus Marine Service. But raw SST alone doesn’t tell you whether a coral is stressed. A reading of 29°C means something very different on the Great Barrier Reef versus Bonaire. What matters isn’t the absolute temperature — it’s how that temperature compares to what the corals at that location have adapted to over their lifetimes.

That realization pushed me toward NOAA CRW’s derived product suite, where that comparison is already built in — and rigorously validated against decades of field bleaching observations worldwide.

The climatological baseline — and why it matters so much

The entire CRW product suite rests on a climatological baseline: a set of long-term average SST values for every location on Earth, for every month of the year. This is not a trivial thing to build correctly. As Skirving et al. (2020) describe, CRW spent years grappling with a fundamental problem — the satellite SST data used to build the climatology came from different sensors and processing pipelines than the near-real-time data used to calculate the anomalies. That mismatch introduced systematic biases, particularly at the high temperatures most relevant to bleaching.

The development of CoralTemp — a single, continuous daily SST record spanning 1985 to the present, built by carefully blending three different satellite products — was specifically designed to solve this problem. For the first time, CRW could compute the climatology and the daily anomalies from the same underlying dataset. The Monthly Mean (MM) climatology was derived from this record across 1985–2012, and the Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM) — the warmest month at each location — became the critical threshold from which the HotSpot and DHW products are derived.

I want to be transparent about why this matters for this project: I am not independently validating the baseline. I am trusting that NOAA CRW’s 25-year investment in getting this right is sound — which is supported by the peer-reviewed literature and by the fact that these products are the global standard used by reef managers worldwide. But understanding that the baseline exists, how it was constructed, and what its assumptions are is essential context for interpreting everything downstream. The DHW values we analyze are only as meaningful as the MMM they’re derived from.

The 5km pixel limitation

One more thing worth acknowledging upfront: the Virtual Station data used in this analysis represents a single 5×5 km satellite pixel per reef location. Real reef systems are far more heterogeneous than that. Temperatures vary with depth, local current patterns, reef orientation, and bathymetry — a coral colony in a deep channel experiences very different conditions than one on a shallow exposed crest, even within the same reef system.

This means the DHW values we analyze are best understood as a reef-wide surface thermal stress index — a strong and validated predictor of bleaching risk at the population level, but not a precise measurement of conditions at any specific location. This is the same limitation the entire global reef monitoring community works with, and it doesn’t undermine the analysis. It just means we should be careful about over-interpreting site-level differences that might reflect local thermal refugia or exposure patterns rather than true differences in bleaching pressure.

With those caveats clearly on the table, here is what this project actually does.

Why This Work Matters to Me

The spikes in the DHW charts are not abstract. Each one represents a mass bleaching event — weeks of accumulated heat stress that caused corals to expel the symbiotic algae that give them both their color and their ability to survive. Some recovered. Many did not.

The 2023 spike on the Florida Keys chart stopped me the first time I saw it in person on a dive. That summer, sea surface temperatures off the Keys reached levels never recorded in the satellite era — DHW values pushing well past the Alert Level 2 threshold where mortality becomes likely. I did not feel unsettled. I felt implicated. Like I was watching something irreplaceable disappear and I was not doing my share to stop it.

We named this planet wrong. It is not Earth. It is Ocean. The ocean covers 71% of the surface, produces more than half the oxygen we breathe, and absorbs the heat that our atmosphere can no longer contain. Coral reefs are less than 1% of the ocean floor and support roughly 25% of all marine species. They are disproportionately vital and disproportionately vulnerable — and they are bleaching at a pace that the satellite record has never seen before.

That feeling of personal responsibility is what brought me here.

I have spent my career in biomedical and clinical research informatics — building data pipelines, managing research data governance, working with complex health datasets in high-stakes environments. The technical skills transfer. What I am building now is the domain knowledge and the credibility to point those skills at a different set of urgent problems. The ocean is not separate from human health. It is the precondition for it.

The monitoring infrastructure that NOAA CRW has built — the same data we are working with in this notebook — exists because early warning matters. A reef manager who knows a bleaching event is coming two weeks in advance can act. That connection between data, prediction, and real-world response is something I understand from clinical informatics. I want to bring it here. I want my work to matter outside the walls of a hospital system.

This project is a first step. The code is simple, the dataset is publicly available, and the science belongs to the researchers at NOAA CRW and the marine biologists doing fieldwork on these reefs. But the ability to work with this data rigorously, document it honestly, and communicate it clearly — that is something I can contribute. And I intend to keep going.

These sites were chosen to represent geographic diversity across two ocean basins, a range of documented bleaching histories, and contrasting thermal stress profiles. Station IDs were verified directly against the live NOAA CRW Virtual Station list at coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/product/vs/data.php before any data was fetched.

A note on the GBR: Unlike the Caribbean sites, the Great Barrier Reef has no single regional station — it is simply too large. NOAA splits it into five sectors: Torres Strait, Far Northern, Northern, Central, and Southern. We selected GBR Central, which covers the Whitsundays and Townsville region. My awareness of the 2016 bleaching event in this sector came initially from watching Jeff Orlowski’s documentary Chasing Coral — and I later found that the same event was the subject of Hughes et al. (2017) in Nature. The documentary and the peer-reviewed literature were pointing at the same place.

Science Background

Sea Surface Temperature Anomaly

Every ocean location has a long-term average temperature for each time of year, derived from decades of satellite records. The SST anomaly measures the departure of today’s temperature from that historical average:

\[\text{SST Anomaly} = \text{SST} - \text{Daily SST Climatology}\]

A positive anomaly means the ocean is warmer than usual for that season. This metric provides broad context for ocean warming but is not on its own a reliable predictor of coral bleaching, because corals in different locations are adapted to different baseline temperatures.

Coral Bleaching HotSpot

The HotSpot product is calibrated specifically to coral biology. It measures how far the current temperature exceeds the warmest month that corals at that location have historically experienced — a threshold called the Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM):

\[\text{HotSpot} = \text{SST} - \text{MMM SST Climatology}, \quad \text{HotSpot} \geq 0\]

A HotSpot ≥ 1°C indicates active thermal stress. The MMM baseline varies by location, so this metric captures relative stress rather than absolute temperature — making it comparable across sites with very different ambient ocean temperatures (Skirving et al. 2020).

Degree Heating Weeks (DHW)

A single warm day can stress corals, but corals are resilient to brief exposures. What causes bleaching is sustained thermal stress accumulating over weeks. Degree Heating Weeks integrate the HotSpot values (when ≥ 1°C) over a moving 12-week window:

\[\text{DHW} = \frac{\sum_{d=0}^{83} \max(\text{HotSpot}_d - 0, 0) \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{HotSpot}_d \geq 1]}{7}\]

Units are °C-weeks. Think of DHW as a “heat debt” that builds up as hot conditions persist — analogous to the accumulated dose concept in clinical pharmacology or radiation biology.

Two key thresholds, established through satellite monitoring and field validation (Liu et al. 2013):

DHW Value Alert Level Biological Expectation
< 4 °C-weeks Watch / Warning Thermal stress present; monitoring needed
≥ 4 °C-weeks Alert Level 1 Bleaching likely
≥ 8 °C-weeks Alert Level 2 Severe bleaching; significant mortality likely

Data & Methods

Data Acquisition

Data were fetched programmatically from the NOAA CRW Virtual Station feed using verified station IDs. The fetch URL pattern is:

https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/product/vs/data/{station_id}.txt

Each file opens with an unstructured metadata header containing station-level information — including the Averaged Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM) temperature, which we extract programmatically and carry through the analysis as the location-specific bleaching threshold baseline.

A note on the file format: The NOAA CRW Virtual Station files are not simple CSVs. The header uses plain text labels rather than # comment markers, the date is split across three columns (YYYY, MM, DD), and the column names reflect the updated v3.1 product suite. We inspected the raw file before writing the parser rather than assuming a format — a step worth documenting for anyone reproducing this analysis.

Data are cached locally after first retrieval. To force a fresh download, set force_refresh=True in the fetch call.

Analysis Pipeline

  1. Fetch — live HTTP request to CRW Virtual Station feed per site
  2. Cache — store raw .txt files in data/raw/
  3. Parse — extract MMM from header, build date column, rename columns
  4. Visualize — DHW time series, alert level distributions, annual peak heatmap
  5. Interpret — narrative analysis of key bleaching events

CRW_BASE_URL = "https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/product/vs/data/{station_id}.txt"

def parse_crw_header(lines):
    """Extract metadata from the CRW file header block."""
    meta = {}
    for i, line in enumerate(lines):
        line_stripped = line.strip()
        if line_stripped == "Averaged Maximum Monthly Mean:":
            meta["mmm"] = float(lines[i + 1].strip())
        if line_stripped == "Name:":
            meta["name"] = lines[i + 1].strip()
        if line_stripped.startswith("YYYY MM DD"):
            meta["data_start_line"] = i
            break
    return meta

def fetch_crw_station(site_name, station_id, force_refresh=False):
    cache_path = os.path.join(CACHE_DIR, f"{station_id}.txt")

    if not force_refresh and os.path.exists(cache_path):
        print(f"  ✓ Loaded from cache: {site_name}")
        with open(cache_path, "r") as f:
            raw_lines = f.readlines()
    else:
        url = CRW_BASE_URL.format(station_id=station_id)
        print(f"  ↓ Fetching live: {site_name}")
        resp = requests.get(url, timeout=30)
        resp.raise_for_status()
        raw_lines = resp.text.splitlines(keepends=True)
        with open(cache_path, "w") as f:
            f.writelines(raw_lines)

    meta = parse_crw_header(raw_lines)
    data_start = meta["data_start_line"]

    data_text = "".join(raw_lines[data_start:])
    df = pd.read_csv(
        StringIO(data_text),
        sep=r"\s+",
        na_values=["-999", "-9999", "-99.0000"],
    )

    df["date"] = pd.to_datetime(
        df["YYYY"].astype(str) + "-" +
        df["MM"].astype(str).str.zfill(2) + "-" +
        df["DD"].astype(str).str.zfill(2)
    )

    df.rename(columns={
        "SST@90th_HS":        "sst",
        "SSTA@90th_HS":       "sst_anomaly",
        "90th_HS>0":          "hotspot",
        "DHW_from_90th_HS>1": "dhw",
        "BAA_7day_max":       "alert",
    }, inplace=True)

    df["site"] = site_name
    df["mmm"]  = meta.get("mmm", None)

    keep = ["date", "sst", "sst_anomaly", "hotspot", "dhw", "alert", "site", "mmm"]
    df = df[[c for c in keep if c in df.columns]]
    df.sort_values("date", inplace=True)
    df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)

    print(f"    ✓ {len(df):,} rows | "
          f"{df['date'].min().date()} → {df['date'].max().date()} | "
          f"MMM: {meta.get('mmm')}°C")
    return df

for site_name, cfg in SITES.items():

Interpretation & Limitations

What this analysis shows

This analysis demonstrates the utility of satellite thermal monitoring for tracking bleaching risk across geographically distributed reef systems. The DHW metric — developed and validated by NOAA CRW scientists over more than 20 years — provides a biologically grounded, globally consistent index of accumulated thermal stress that has been correlated with field observations of bleaching onset and severity.

Limitations to note

  • Point data: CRW Virtual Station data represent a single 5×5 km pixel per site. Actual reef conditions vary at finer spatial scales due to bathymetry, currents, and local upwelling.
  • No in situ validation: This analysis does not include field bleaching surveys or coral cover data. DHW is a risk indicator, not a direct measurement of bleaching.
  • Alert thresholds are global: The 4 and 8 °C-week thresholds are population- level averages. Some coral communities may have higher or lower thermal tolerance.
  • GBR representation: The gbr_central station represents one of five GBR sectors. Thermal stress patterns vary considerably across the reef’s 2,300 km length.

Future Directions

This MVP analysis establishes the data pipeline and visualization framework. Planned extensions include:

  • All five GBR sectors — compare Torres Strait, Far Northern, Northern, Central, and Southern to examine latitudinal bleaching gradients
  • Spatial raster analysis — process global CRW NetCDF files via ERDDAP to produce regional DHW maps
  • Bleaching observation integration — overlay NOAA CoRIS or Reef Check field survey data to validate satellite signals against in-water observations
  • ENSO correlation — correlate DHW peaks with Niño 3.4 SST anomaly index
  • Trend analysis — fit linear trend models to annual peak DHW to quantify warming trajectories at each site

1985 to the present, built by carefully blending three different satellite products
— was specifically designed to solve this problem. For the first time, CRW could
compute the climatology and the daily anomalies from the *same* underlying dataset.
The Monthly Mean (MM) climatology was derived from this record across 1985–2012,
and the **Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM)** — the warmest month at each location —
became the critical threshold from which the HotSpot and DHW products are derived.

I want to be transparent about why this matters for this project: I am not
independently validating the baseline. I am trusting that NOAA CRW's 25-year
investment in getting this right is sound — which is supported by the peer-reviewed
literature and by the fact that these products are the global standard used by reef
managers worldwide. But understanding *that the baseline exists, how it was
constructed, and what its assumptions are* is essential context for interpreting
everything downstream. The DHW values we analyze are only as meaningful as the MMM
they're derived from.

### The 5km pixel limitation

One more thing worth acknowledging upfront: the Virtual Station data used in this
analysis represents a single 5×5 km satellite pixel per reef location. Real reef
systems are far more heterogeneous than that. Temperatures vary with depth, local
current patterns, reef orientation, and bathymetry — a coral colony in a deep
channel experiences very different conditions than one on a shallow exposed crest,
even within the same reef system.

This means the DHW values we analyze are best understood as a **reef-wide surface
thermal stress index** — a strong and validated predictor of bleaching risk at the
population level, but not a precise measurement of conditions at any specific
location. This is the same limitation the entire global reef monitoring community
works with, and it doesn't undermine the analysis. It just means we should be
careful about over-interpreting site-level differences that might reflect local
thermal refugia or exposure patterns rather than true differences in bleaching
pressure.

With those caveats clearly on the table, here is what this project actually does.

## Science Background

### Sea Surface Temperature Anomaly

Every ocean location has a long-term average temperature for each time of year,
derived from decades of satellite records. The **SST anomaly** measures the
departure of today's temperature from that historical average:

$$\text{SST Anomaly} = \text{SST} - \text{Daily SST Climatology}$$

A positive anomaly means the ocean is warmer than usual for that season. This
metric provides broad context for ocean warming but is not on its own a reliable
predictor of coral bleaching, because corals in different locations are adapted to
different baseline temperatures.

### Coral Bleaching HotSpot

The **HotSpot** product is calibrated specifically to coral biology. It measures
how far the current temperature exceeds the *warmest month* that corals at that
location have historically experienced — a threshold called the Maximum Monthly
Mean (MMM):

$$\text{HotSpot} = \text{SST} - \text{MMM SST Climatology}, \quad \text{HotSpot} \geq 0$$

A HotSpot ≥ 1°C indicates active thermal stress. The MMM baseline varies by
location, so this metric captures *relative* stress rather than absolute
temperature — making it comparable across sites with very different ambient ocean
temperatures [@skirving2020].

### Degree Heating Weeks (DHW)

A single warm day can stress corals, but corals are resilient to brief exposures.
What causes bleaching is *sustained* thermal stress accumulating over weeks.
**Degree Heating Weeks** integrate the HotSpot values (when ≥ 1°C) over a moving
12-week window:

$$\text{DHW} = \frac{\sum_{d=0}^{83} \max(\text{HotSpot}_d - 0, 0) \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{HotSpot}_d \geq 1]}{7}$$

Units are **°C-weeks**. Think of DHW as a "heat debt" that builds up as hot
conditions persist — analogous to the accumulated dose concept in clinical
pharmacology or radiation biology.

Two key thresholds, established through satellite monitoring and field validation
[@liu2013]:

| DHW Value | Alert Level | Biological Expectation |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| < 4 °C-weeks | Watch / Warning | Thermal stress present; monitoring needed |
| ≥ 4 °C-weeks | **Alert Level 1** | Bleaching likely |
| ≥ 8 °C-weeks | **Alert Level 2** | Severe bleaching; significant mortality likely |

## Data & Methods

### Data Acquisition

Data were fetched programmatically from the NOAA CRW Virtual Station feed using
verified station IDs. The fetch URL pattern is:

```
https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/product/vs/data/{station_id}.txt
```

Each file opens with an unstructured metadata header containing station-level
information — including the **Averaged Maximum Monthly Mean (MMM)** temperature,
which we extract programmatically and carry through the analysis as the
location-specific bleaching threshold baseline.

::: {.callout-note appearance="simple"}
**A note on the file format:** The NOAA CRW Virtual Station files are not simple
CSVs. The header uses plain text labels rather than `#` comment markers, the date
is split across three columns (`YYYY`, `MM`, `DD`), and the column names reflect
the updated v3.1 product suite. We inspected the raw file before writing the parser
rather than assuming a format — a step worth documenting for anyone reproducing
this analysis.
:::

Data are cached locally after first retrieval. To force a fresh download, set
`force_refresh=True` in the fetch call.

### Analysis Pipeline

1. **Fetch** — live HTTP request to CRW Virtual Station feed per site
2. **Cache** — store raw `.txt` files in `data/raw/`
3. **Parse** — extract MMM from header, build date column, rename columns
4. **Visualize** — DHW time series, alert level distributions, annual peak heatmap
5. **Interpret** — narrative analysis of key bleaching events

```{python}
#| label: fetch
#| code-summary: "Fetch and parse NOAA CRW Virtual Station data"

CRW_BASE_URL = "https://coralreefwatch.noaa.gov/product/vs/data/{station_id}.txt"

def parse_crw_header(lines):
    """Extract metadata from the CRW file header block."""
    meta = {}
    for i, line in enumerate(lines):
        line_stripped = line.strip()
        if line_stripped == "Averaged Maximum Monthly Mean:":
            meta["mmm"] = float(lines[i + 1].strip())
        if line_stripped == "Name:":
            meta["name"] = lines[i + 1].strip()
        if line_stripped.startswith("YYYY MM DD"):
            meta["data_start_line"] = i
            break
    return meta

def fetch_crw_station(site_name, station_id, force_refresh=False):
    cache_path = os.path.join(CACHE_DIR, f"{station_id}.txt")

    if not force_refresh and os.path.exists(cache_path):
        print(f"  ✓ Loaded from cache: {site_name}")
        with open(cache_path, "r") as f:
            raw_lines = f.readlines()
    else:
        url = CRW_BASE_URL.format(station_id=station_id)
        print(f"  ↓ Fetching live: {site_name}")
        resp = requests.get(url, timeout=30)
        resp.raise_for_status()
        raw_lines = resp.text.splitlines(keepends=True)
        with open(cache_path, "w") as f:
            f.writelines(raw_lines)

    meta = parse_crw_header(raw_lines)
    data_start = meta["data_start_line"]

    data_text = "".join(raw_lines[data_start:])
    df = pd.read_csv(
        StringIO(data_text),
        sep=r"\s+",
        na_values=["-999", "-9999", "-99.0000"],
    )

    df["date"] = pd.to_datetime(
        df["YYYY"].astype(str) + "-" +
        df["MM"].astype(str).str.zfill(2) + "-" +
        df["DD"].astype(str).str.zfill(2)
    )

    df.rename(columns={
        "SST@90th_HS":        "sst",
        "SSTA@90th_HS":       "sst_anomaly",
        "90th_HS>0":          "hotspot",
        "DHW_from_90th_HS>1": "dhw",
        "BAA_7day_max":       "alert",
    }, inplace=True)

    df["site"] = site_name
    df["mmm"]  = meta.get("mmm", None)

    keep = ["date", "sst", "sst_anomaly", "hotspot", "dhw", "alert", "site", "mmm"]
    df = df[[c for c in keep if c in df.columns]]
    df.sort_values("date", inplace=True)
    df.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)

    print(f"    ✓ {len(df):,} rows | "
          f"{df['date'].min().date()} → {df['date'].max().date()} | "
          f"MMM: {meta.get('mmm')}°C")
    return df


print("Fetching NOAA CRW Virtual Station data...\n")
all_data = {}
for site_name, cfg in SITES.items():
    df = fetch_crw_station(site_name, cfg["station_id"])
    all_data[site_name] = df

print(f"\n✓ Done. {len(all_data)} sites loaded.")
```

## Interpretation & Limitations

### What this analysis shows

This analysis demonstrates the utility of satellite thermal monitoring for
tracking bleaching risk across geographically distributed reef systems. The DHW
metric — developed and validated by NOAA CRW scientists over more than 20 years —
provides a biologically grounded, globally consistent index of accumulated thermal
stress that has been correlated with field observations of bleaching onset and
severity.

### Limitations to note

- **Point data:** CRW Virtual Station data represent a single 5×5 km pixel per
  site. Actual reef conditions vary at finer spatial scales due to bathymetry,
  currents, and local upwelling.
- **No in situ validation:** This analysis does not include field bleaching surveys
  or coral cover data. DHW is a *risk indicator*, not a direct measurement of
  bleaching.
- **Alert thresholds are global:** The 4 and 8 °C-week thresholds are population-
  level averages. Some coral communities may have higher or lower thermal tolerance.
- **GBR representation:** The `gbr_central` station represents one of five GBR
  sectors. Thermal stress patterns vary considerably across the reef's 2,300 km
  length.
